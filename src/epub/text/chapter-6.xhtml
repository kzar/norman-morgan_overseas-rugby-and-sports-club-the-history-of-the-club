During the meeting the Hon. Secretary, Mr. C. Vivian Morris, paid tribute to Major Scott Lowe who had been President of the Club for two seasons. He expressed the wish of all Members that Major Scott Lowe would be speedily returned to good health in his retirement.

In November, 1960, in appreciation of his services to “The Overseas” Cmdr. E. Hamilton-Hill (Retd.) was made an Hon. Member of the Club for the 1960/61 season. Commander Hamilton-Hill had been President of the Club for 9 years, 1947 to 1956. In a letter to Mr. C. V. Morris, Cmdr. Hamilton-Hill commented on the number of years he had been associated with the Club. Indeed, over the years, as Managing Director of Rediffusion (Malta) Limited, he never failed to comply with a request by the Club to broadcast information, especially reference the A.G.M., over the rediffusion network.

Club House - Marsa Lodge

At the end of the 1959/60 season, although the Club House at Gerry’s Bar just managed to pay for itself, in spite of the high rent, the search for a new home began. In the close season of 1960 arrangements were being made to have premises at Rocky Vale, Spinola, St. Julians, as a Club House. Fortunately,as it turned out, these arrangements fell through. At the A.G.M. on the 10th October, 1960, the President, Lt. Col. T. G. Chambers, promised he would enquire into the possibility of having army premises. Immediately, Capt. John Day, Secretary Army Rugby Union (Malta) and Captain of the Nomads R.F.C., became actively engaged in looking for a suitable Club House for the Nomads/Overseas. On the 18th October, 1960, Lt. Col. Chambers wrote to the Overseas Hon. Secretary, Vivian Morris, informing him that Capt. Day had received permission to use a club room at Marsa (Marsa Lodge).

At an Overseas Committee Meeting on the 27th October, 1960, John Day outlined the facilities which were available at Marsa Lodge and said he would arrange for furnishings and bar fittings to be installed. He pointed out that the Nomads would probably have some difficulty in running the Club themselves and thought that the General Management of the Club and Bar could be better organized by the Overseas in view of their Social and Bar Committee organization. Needless to say, the Overseas Committee accepted the kind offer. At the same meeting it was decided that the running of the Bar should be undertaken by a joint Bar Committee of Overseas and Nomads. Messrs. B. Headland (Nomads) and S. Ward (R. Sigs) were elected as Associate Members of the Club House Committee to supervise Club Bar, particularly for mid week games. It was agreed that all money from Bar sales should be handed to the Hon. Treasurer and put into the Overseas account in Barclays. The first Bar Manager was Mr. T. Goswell.

The Marsa Lodge was to be “home” for Overseas for 11½ years, in fact until the British Forces withdrawal in January 1972. The formal opening of the Club House was on the 3rd December, 1960, when H.M.S. Ausonia beat Overseas 17 - 0 in a practice game. The situation was ideal - a Club House within the playing area. As Vivian Morris printed in a rugby news letter dated the 6th March, 1961, the Club House quickly became the rugger centre on the island. The Club House consisted of a large Bar room and an equally large second room normally closed off from the Bar room by a sliding partition. This second room was used on occasions for dances and many other types of functions. On two sides of the building were changing rooms and showers which Overseas teams could use on request. It was decided that the Club House would be opened on a Saturday or Sunday afternoon after an Overseas game at the Marsa, and on request by one of the Service teams during the week, normally on a Wednesday. During the first season the normal Club Night was on a Wednesday evening when many functions were arranged to make it popular and going concern. From the second season onwards the normal Club Night was on a Thursday evening. The Nomads also held functions separately or in conjunction with the Overseas.

The Maltese caretaker, “George”, was an army pensioner who lived in a small cottage behind the Club House. The Overseas “inherited” George with the Club buildings and he soon became part of the Overseas. It was a little difficult to communicate because of language problems but we managed. The Overseas Committee paid him £1 a week to clean the Club rooms. In January, 1965, this was raised to £2 a week. After a game George was always there clearing glasses away from which he earned the princely sum of 2/6d. George was “retired” by the army in October, 1967, and consequently he was forced to vacate his cottage and so he had to leave us.

On the 16th December, 1960, Tony Goswell resigned as Bar Manager. As a Flying Officer in the R.A.F. his duties often took him away from Malta. Mr. D. Isted became the New Bar Manager.

Novel methods were used to decorate the Club rooms. On the 3rd February, 1961, Capt. J. C. Griffiths, R.A.O.C., on behalf of the Club, wrote to approximately 36 well known rugby clubs in U.K., including Twickenham and Murrayfield, asking for any old rugby posters advertising matches to decorate the club room walls and give them a rugby flavour. I do not know what success he had. John Griffiths was co-opted onto the Bar Committee on the 1st February, 1961. At a Club dance held at the Marsa Lodge on the 28th January, 1961, the walls were gaily decorated with rugger jerseys of various teams on the island.

On the 11th January, 1961, the Bar Manager opened a separate Bar account at Barclays. However, at the end of June, 1961, the Bar and General accounts at Barclays were made into one. For the first season the Bar net profits were shared with the Nomads R.F.C. and for this purpose the Bar account was audited up to the 23rd March, 1961. The net profit to each Club was £19.

On the 15th June, 1961, the Overseas Committee decided that in future the Bar profits would be shared between Overseas and the Joint Services Rugby Sports Fund. The period during which the profits would be shared ran from the 1st October to the 31st March, i.e. the playing season. The Overseas Club would make the Club room available during this period for any Social/Playing functions. Capt. J. Day, army representative on the Overseas General Committee, was the prime mover of this decision and so it must have been the wish of the army authorities that profits be shared in this way.

On the 24th September, 1961, Lt. Col. T. G. Chambers, as Chairman Army Rugby (Malta), wrote to Mr. C. V. Morris, Hon. Secretary Overseas, setting out the conditions required for Overseas to continue to use Marsa Lodge. This communication was referred to as Sigs/426. Lt. Col. Chambers asked that the following condition be recorded in the minutes of the next Overseas A.G.M.

“A formal request must be made annually on behalf of the Overseas Rugby Club (Malta) for the continued use of the Marsa Club House by the Overseas Rugby Club. The Chairman Army Rugby (Malta) will in turn approach the General Officer Commanding and will communicate to the Overseas Rugby Club the General Officer Commanding’s decision. The request should be made in September of each year and will cover the period 1st October to 30th September following receipt of the request.”

“The first request will be due in September, 1962.”

Sigs/426 remained in force until the 3rd November, 1967, when the Overseas were allowed to use Marsa Lodge as an “Encroachment”. More of that later.

In consequence of the different method of sharing profits the representatives of the three Services were asked to sit on the Overseas Bar Management Committee in lieu of the Nomad R.F.C. representatives. These Service representatives were elected at the A.G.M. They were given authority by the Overseas full Committee to open the Club House on occasions when an elected member of the Bar Committee was not present for Service games.

Each year, as soon as possible after the 31st March, an Extraordinary General Meeting was held to present the Bar account for the 6 months ending the 31st March. The Bar and General Accounts were audited for the year on the 30th September. On the occasion of the Bar account for the 6 months ending the 31st March being presented, a cheque for the Joint Services Sports Board’s share of the net Bar profits was handed to the Secretary Army R.U. (Malta). The Secretary Army R.U. in turn shared this amount between the three Services via the Joint Service Sports Board. After a difference of opinion between the Overseas Club and the Secretary of the J.S.S.B. in May, 1963, this method of payment of the Services’ share of the profits was changed. The difference of opinion was due to the fact that the Secretary Army R.U. (Malta) had approached the Overseas Hon. Secretary before the end of the 1962/63 playing season and asked for an advance of approximately £40 to assist with the expenses incurred by the army rugby team which visited Tripoli early 1963. The Secretary J.S.S.B. objected on the grounds that Inter Service Sports Board had not been approached for, nor approved, this expenditure. All ended well, but from the 31st March, 1964 each Service representative was presented with an individual cheque for a third of the J.S.S.B.’s share of the profits.

The drop in profit for 1964 was due to a number of reasons including an increase in Bar Stewards wages, small profit margin and less consumption (impossible). The last two mentioned reasons were remedied.

During the 1961/62 season we obtained the services of Mr. Victor Dimech as a paid Bar Steward. Victor served the Club for 4 years before he left Malta on the 4th October, 1965 to live in U.K. He was succeeded as Bar Steward by Mr Angelo Galea who served the Club until it finally closed. In 1964 it is recorded that the Steward’s wage was 3/- an hour and overtime as 4/- an hour. It is also recorded that the hours of opening were as follows:- Thursday, 7.30 p.m. (Steward 7 p.m.)⁠—12 midnight. If a Committee Member was present, and the Members present wished, The Club House could remain open until 1.30 a.m. After midnight the Members present had to pay a levy of not less than a total of 6/- to pay the Steward. Wednesday. When requested by a Service team, 3 p.m. to 7 p.m. Saturday, 2.30 p.m.(Steward 1.30 p.m.) to 7 p.m. Sunday, 4.30 p.m. to 6.30 p.m. Need I say that the Club rarely closed before 1.30 a.m. on a Friday morning. One of the most hilarious games played on a Thursday night was liar dice. How the intellectuals at this game managed to plan and catch an unwary member two and a half times round the table I do not know. I also do not know how some of the schoolmasters taught the next morning - I believe they had some understanding pupils.

In 1964 it was estimated that something in the order of twenty fifteens made use of the Club House each week during the playing season. By this time the original furniture was worn out and sadly in need of replacement. In December, 1964, we purchased surplus furniture from the S.N.S.O., Naval Stores, Malta.

With the Club House and Bar being open three or four times a week during the playing season and with all labour being voluntary, except for the Bar Steward, it is obvious that there were problems in safeguarding the bar takings, especially during the busy periods on Wednesday and Saturday afternoons. Whilst Victor Dimech was the Bar Steward he accepted responsibility. When Angelo Galea became Steward, however, he was not willing to accept responsibility for the takings in the same way, and a new system had to be devised by Ralph McGillivray, Bar Manager (1964/66), and Peter Bennie, Treasurer (1965/67). Individual volunteer members of the Bar Committee agreed to accept responsibility for a complete week at a time, to look after the cash and to pay the week’s bar takings into any Branch of the National Bank of Malta to the account of Overseas R.F.C. A special sheet of “Responsibilities” and “Bar Takings Statement” forms were devised for the Duty Bar Member, copies of which follow. It was agreed that this system was not foolproof, especially during busy periods, but if stocktakings were carried out thoroughly it was easier for checks to be made, and the systematic weekly entries and banking made life easier for the Treasurer.

In September, 1967, came the first major change in conditions required for the Overseas to continue to use the Marsa Lodge as a Club House and this completely cancelled Sigs/426 which had been in force since September, 1961. Our annual request for permission to continue using Marsa Lodge, dated 22nd September, 1967, was sent as usual to the Secretary Army Rugby Football (Malta). Our letter was passed on to H.Q. “Q” Branch and on the 3rd November, 1967, we received the following reply from Mr. J. H. H. Parker of this branch.

“Authority is hereby given for you to hold the Club Room at Marsa Lodge as an Encroachment. This will mean that in accordance with current Ministry of Defence instructions, you will have to pay a ‘peppercorn’ rent and take out an Insurance Policy to cover loss or damage to the building and third party indemnity.”

On the 11th January, 1968, the D.L.A. informed us that we must insure the building for £5,000. On the 28th January, 1968, Mr. Parker (Col. retired) informed Overseas that the Malta Government wished to use the large (2nd) room for coaching athletics between the 1st June and the 31st August, 1968, as a Pilot scheme for the Council of Europe. If we objected we could only have the encroachment for the “Bar Room”. The Maltese Government would also take out an Insurance.

Naturally, we agreed to the conditions required for us to hold the Club Rooms as an encroachment, taking out the necessary insurance. The Bar Manager, Mr. B. Jackson, received the encroachment (Licence) in March, 1968. Initially, we agreed in Committee to continue to share the playing season bar profits with the three Services, but this decision was rescinded at an Extraordinary Meeting, attended by the three Inter Service Representatives; on the 23rd May, 1968. It was then agreed to cease payment of 50% of the playing season bar profit to the Services. Accordingly, at the A.G.M. on the 12th September, 1968, Rule 9 was amended to leave out reference to the Services and also to the Extraordinary Meeting which had previously been held in April for submission of the playing season bar account to members and Joint Service Rugby Representatives.

Under the terms of the encroachment we could not let the Forces Driving Club of Malta (or similar clubs) use the Club House for a function organized by them. They had to apply direct to the D.L.A. Previously we had allowed the Driving Club to use the Club House on certain occasions.

During the summer months of 1961 (April to October) the Club House was kept open on Thursday evenings. Functions were arranged fortnightly although it is not recorded how successful they were. From 1962 to 1964 inclusive the Club House was closed during the summer months for varying periods and even when open there was a loss on the Bar. In 1965 a summer Committee was formed with Mr. P. Ratcliffe (Ratty), Chairman, Mr. B. Bush, Bar Manager, and Mr. B. Jackson, Treasurer. The Club House was kept open throughout the summer months and it was an unqualified success. There was a profit on the Bar of £31. Ben Bush was a born organizer. During the summers of 1966 and 1967 the Club was kept open and facilities were given to the Cricket Section.

In April, 1970, we were informed by the Lands Agent that a Service or Service sponsored Civilian must be responsible as a member of the Committee in order to sign the lease. During this same period we had the opportunity of signing a Licence, drafted by the Lands Agent, which would have given us Security of Tenure for the premises even though the lands were handed back to the Maltese Government. Arrangements were made for Overseas Hon. Secretary, Mr. T. N. Ricketts, and Treasurer, Mr. G. Sharkey, to meet the Lands Agent in order to sign suitable documents to gain complete control of the Club Rooms, although there is no record of this taking place. The next record was dated the 15th October, 1970, when Mr. Sharkey was asked by Overseas Committee to look into the matter as regards Security of Tenure. However, Security of Tenure or otherwise, the matter was settled in no uncertain terms when the British Forces temporarily withdrew from Malta in January, 1972. Marsa Lodge as a Club House was lost to Overseas.

Playing Season 1960/61

In the words of Francis Chapelle, Team Captain, Overseas had quite a good playing record and exceeded expectations during the 1960/61 season. Viv Morris, Hon. Secretary and Team Secretary, said that it had been a successful season on and off the field.

With three exceptions, games were played at the Marsa on the newly renovated grounds. Two games were at Ta’ Qali and one away game at Hal Far. This season proved exceptionally hot, and lack of rain curtailed the number of games drastically. The first game was not until the 10th December, and the last was on the 11th March.

The problem of getting Service teams to play on a Sunday, although always with us, seemed more acute in this season. Whilst it was suggested by Viv Morris, as the previous Fixture Secretary, at the A.G.M., that in view of the highly organized state of Service rugby and the difficulty of playing Associate or Full Service Members on a Saturday, it would be better to organize as many games on a Sunday as possible, this proved difficult. Frank Kitson, Fixture Secretary, was unable to persuade Service teams to play on a Sunday, although he had some hope against visiting ships. In fact, there were only two or three Sunday games throughout this season, and they were against H.M.S. Girdleness and R.N. School (Tal Handaq). I think this particular season stresses the difficulties Overseas faces in putting out a strong rugby XV without cooperation between Overseas and the Service teams. Having said this, I must stress that this cooperation, almost without exception, existed.

It is of interest that Overseas played 2 games against Tal Handaq, losing the first 3⁠–⁠5 and winning the second 9⁠–⁠3. These games were played in good spirit in spite of the fact that the lads were tickled pink to suggest that they would get their own back for all detentions suffered. The “Old Crocks” liked to get an outing in these games and this year was no exception. In the first game Bill Polkinghorne and Viv Morris trotted out onto the field and had to be almost carried off.

During this season the Overseas applied to the Secretary Army R.U. (Malta) to take part in the Inter Service 7’s Tournament. On this occasion it was refused.

Social Events

Although end of season accounts speak of expenses for two dances during the 1960/61 season, the minutes record plans for only one, that of the 28th January, 1961. This is particularly interesting because, although organized by the Overseas Committee, including army representatives, on the dance tickets the following was printed, “The Army Rugby Club requests the pleasure” etc. One must remember, that during this season only, some functions were combined Overseas/Nomads efforts. In fact, in an open letter to Club Members dated the 6th March, 1961, Overseas Secretary, Mr. C. V. Morris, said that social events arranged during the season included an Overseas dance and a Combined Overseas, Forts and Nomads R.F.C.’s dance. I assume that the combined dance mentioned was that held on the 28th January, which was an unqualified success.

Mr. J. George, Hon. Social Secretary, resigned on the 29th March, 1961, prior to leaving Malta. He was succeeded by Mr. J. C. Griffiths who made strenuous efforts to put Overseas on a good social footing during the summer months. The Club House was kept open on Thursday evenings throughout the close season with fortnightly functions arranged. John Griffiths himself left Malta in July, 1961.

Officials Leaving Malta

In July, 1961, two members who had been part of the Social life of Overseas for three years, Bill and May Polkinghorne, left Malta. Both gave unstintingly in time and effort. Bill had been Hon. Treasurer for 3 years 1958⁠–⁠61, Club House/Bar Manager 1958/59, and in spite of having previously hung up his boots once or twice, he was the regular full back for Overseas in 1958/59. Bill had previously been a playing member of Overseas between 1950⁠–⁠53. May had been on the Social Committee in 1958/59 and again in 1960/61, and was the last Lady Committee Member to be elected at the A.G.M.

John George left Malta in August, 1961, after being Social Secretary in 1958/59 and again in 1960/61. In 1958/59 he organized events with very little financial backing and on this score he must have found it easier in 1960/61. John was an invaluable member of any Committee and his support and advice was welcomed by three Hon. Secretaries. He organized much of the printing required by Overseas between 1958⁠–⁠61.
