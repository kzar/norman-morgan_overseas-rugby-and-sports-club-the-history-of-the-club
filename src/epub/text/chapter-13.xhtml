The President, Mr. J.P. Ratcliffe, pointed out that although the rules stated that the A.G.M. be held in October, the present A.G.M. was being held earlier, as was intended by the previous amendment, concerning the ending of the financial year, carried at the A.G.M. 1966. The President asked members to accept this and that a resolution to alter the rules would be proposed at this A.G.M. Having the A.G.M. in September, instead of October, allowed the Team Captain, elected at the A.G.M., to start training earlier.

The following resolution to amend Rule 9 was proposed and passed:

"Annual General Meeting to be held in September, and an Extra-ordinary Meeting to be held in April for submission of the playing season bar account to members and Joint Service Rugby Representatives. The financial year to end on the 31st March."

Honorary Life Members

Honorary Life Members of the Overseas R.U.F.C., which is an honour bestowed on members by direct voting at an A.G.M. in recognition of outstanding service to the Club, was initiated at the A.G.M., 1967. The first Life Members, Mr. J.A. Sharp and Mr. C.V. Morris, were made at this A.G.M. and to the present date only seven members have received this honour. I do not propose to give the individual service which each member gave to merit Life Membership, I think the granting of such an honour speaks for itself.

At this point I must remind readers that the first Honorary Life Memberships were granted whilst the Club was known as the Overseas R.U.F.C., hence the wording of the first paragraph under the heading 'Honorary Life Membership'. At the A.G.M. of 1973, when the Club became known as the Overseas Rugby and Sports Club, the honours already granted were automatically transferred under the new name. Under both names special Membership Cards were printed.

Playing Season

The 1967/68 playing season proved to be one of the most outstanding in the Club's history. The 'Overseas' Tour to Messina was undoubtedly the highlight, followed closely by the Inter Service 7 a-side Competition, and the end of season tours to Malta by the R.A.F. Cyprus; Nomads (Tripoli); John Evans XV; and the return visit of Messina University. Overseas played 30 games, a record in one season, and scored 434 points to 193. The Team Captain, Brian Jackson, mentioned no particular player's prowess on the field, but he left a very comprehensive analysis of fixtures, player's appearances, and individual scores. He mentioned Jack Handford and David Brooks leading the forwards, and the general fitness of Overseas players, thanks to the excellent work of Trevor Ricketts on Thursday nights (Training sessions). A very thorough record of the Tour made by Overseas remains. 'Jacko' sums it up by saying, it was a very successful season.

On the 24th November, 1967, as Secretary, I wrote to the Sports Editor of the Sunday Telegraph, London, asking if it was possible for the results of our games to be printed in the Rugby Union results column of the Sunday Telegraph. On different occasions Overseas Committee members had been asked for this service by members returning to U.K. Our first reply from the Sports Editor said that this was not feasible as they could not give results which were received 2 days old, never mind a week. We then received agreement, that if we could get the results to London on Saturday evenings, and then phone them immediately to the Sunday Telegraph Office, they would be accepted. However, this proved impracticable and so the matter was dropped.

The Tour to Messina was arranged as a result of an article in 'Rugby World' describing the 'Penguins' tour to Malta in May the previous year. On reading this article the Secretary of Messina University Rugby Club, or to be correct, Centro Universitario Sportivo, Messina, Sicily, wrote to Overseas suggesting we visit Messina, and that they pay a return visit at a later date. The Centro Universitario Sportivo, known as CUS, had initially thought we were a Maltese Club. The Tour was agreed to, and on the 7th December, 1967, a Sub-Committee was formed to make arrangements.

The Tour to Messina took place between the 1st and 5th January, 1968. For the crossing from Malta to Syracuse on board the Citta di Alessandria we had booked an 18 berth dormitory, just about the size of an average cabin, plus a 3 or 4 berth cabin. We had a bus waiting on the quayside to transport us the 93 miles to Messina where we were met at the Central Station by the CUS Secretary, Pino di Lorenzo. The hospitality of the Sicilians was tremendous. As guests in Student House, or Casa dello Studente, each member of our party had an individual bed sitter. Being the Christmas holiday period, all the students and staff were away on holiday, and so we were fed, at CUS expense, at a nearby cafe. At most meals we were joined by the CUS Secretary, or the CUS President, Piero Jaci. For one meal we were joined by the whole Sicilian team. On this occasion, helped by a liberal amount of wine, we finished the meal by singing rugby songs, English and Italian. Neither team understood the other team's songs, but if Italian rugby songs are like English rugby songs, and the 'entente cordiale' was such that it seemed certain they were, then I don't know why we were not thrown out of the cafe. However, everyone, including staff and passers-by enjoyed themselves.

In the evening of the first day we were entertained by Members of the University Rugby Club at their Club House, where we were presented with a Sicilian Carroza and plied with Asti Spumante and cake. The following morning we were given a pleasant reception at the main Messina University by the Rector, Il Magnifico Rettore, Professor Salvatore Pugliatti. All Overseas Members were given a commemorative medallion. The 4th January, being a free day, we went by bus with most of our hosts to Taormina.

The CUS rugby pitch, if one can describe it as such, was within the walls of the old 'Cittadella', which is situated near the harbour. The 'pitch' is best described in the Team Captain's words.

"One of the most memorable moments of the tour was undoubtedly our first glimpse of the 'pitch', as it came into view between the cranes and piles of rusting scrap metal which littered the Messina dockside. No-one could believe it! It was hopefully stated that that must be their practice ground, until the coach pulled up outside dressing rooms which made the Marsa seem palatial and far distant. We have been warned that the pitch was not in very good condition owing to the heavy rain, but the mud bath we had expected turned out to be a stretch of gravel half covered by large areas of standing water. Grey skies and persistent rain did not help to improve the picture. In fairness it should be stated, however, that Messina does have an adequate stadium, which unfortunately, and to our hosts' dismay, was unplayable because of the bad weather."

We were beaten, just, in both games played. We had the obvious handicaps, such as the inability of some of the regular Overseas 1st team players to tour, the pitch, and perhaps, above all, the difference in interpretation of the rules by the Sicilians and ourselves. On the other hand we were up against a good team, continental style, with their forwards as ready to run with the ball as their threequarters. Throughout the first game it rained incessantly with an icy wind blowing straight down from the mountains. For the second game the weather was a little kinder, but still wet and windy with water lying over large areas of the gravel pitch. All Overseas team played well, with P. Parker; P. Miller; J. Handford; T. Ricketts and C. Rice being mentioned in the Captain's dispatches. Peter Parker received well earned praise in the local Messina press.

During both games, the Sicilian players arrived on and left the pitch with such startling rapidity that it seemed scarcely possible that they had only two substitutes. It is customary in Italy to allow two substitutes. On one occasion, during the first game whilst a line out was taking place, there were 19 Sicilians on the field, including the Referee and Trainer. Overseas players were tickled pink, they could scarcely believe it. Two games full of excitement.

In the first game Brian Jackson badly grazed his rear quarters by sliding on the treacherous surface, very painful and very embarrassing. In spite of being in considerable pain Brian played in the second game, much to the relief of the team and selectors. Even with Brian we had only 17 players. The second game was marred by unfortunate injuries to Reg Squires, broken collar bone, and Mike Perkins, broken ankle. Though uncomfortable, they both made the journey back with the party in the bus.

All members of the touring party thoroughly enjoyed the tour, the games, the hospitality, and the people. For the 3 days it was constantly raining or drizzling and we all returned to Malta with huge black umbrellas bought in Messina – a future symbol of our Tour status. Our President, Peter Ratcliffe, was henceforth known as 'Il Magnifico Presidente', and our battle cry for future games during that season at the Marsa was 'Forza' and 'Avanti'. Only Peter Ratcliffe and John Why could speak any Italian and we were constantly relying on their good offices. The total cost of the Tour to each member of the touring party was only £10.3s7d.

The game against the Tripoli Nomads was played on No. 3 pitch, Marsa and those against the CUS and John Evans XV were played on the Stadium pitch, Marsa. Playing so late in the season presented difficulties with dry and hard grounds. Thanks must go to Mr. Hanlon, Sports Field, M.P.B.W., for caring for and constant watering of the pitches. The referees were provided by the Referees Panel, Malta, Secretary, Staff Sgt. W.J. Thomas.

The Nomads, R.F.C. from Tripoli, Libya (13 in party) stopped at St. Julians Hotel, Paceville, at their own expense. Overseas provided transport. The Tour was arranged for the Nomads, initially, by Secretary, Mr. D. Webb, and later arrangements by Secretary, Mr. R. Harrison. Unfortunately, 5 of their players were unable to make the tour due to Visa problems at the Tripoli end, and they were further handicapped by injury problems on the tour. F.O. L.C.V. Hill, Secretary R.A.F. (Malta) R.U., helped the tourists by providing players to complete their team. In spite of the high scores against them, in the words of the local correspondent in the Times of Malta, the Nomads provided fine sport and Bill Dunlop's Trojan efforts in their back row was the highlight of their play.

The Nomads were followed by the CUS, Messina, on the return visit (19 in party). CUS stopped at Mount St. Joseph House, Targa Gap, Mosta, with expenses paid by Overseas. Overseas provided transport. Like all rugby teams, CUS enjoyed life, and I think Father Darmanin, Principal of Mount St. Joseph House, was very understanding. The Sicilians played good rugby and this time, in two very exciting open games, the honours were even. Overseas tried, as far as possible, to play the same team as that which visited Messina. Looking back, Overseas/CUS tours and games proved a very interesting part of Overseas history.

Finally, we had the tour of John Evans' party from South Wales. John, an Ex Overseas player, brought an exceptionally talented party of players, including Derek Morgan (England and Barbarians). This was a memorable tour. They were billeted at 234 Signal Squadron Mess, Lintorn Barracks, Floriana, by kind permission of Major Brian Woods, at a very moderate cost to the tourists. Arrangements for accommodation and transport were made by Capt. Mike Adams, Secretary, Army (Malta) R.U. In two extremely hard fought games the visitors remained undefeated. Both games were dominated by the outstanding play and leadership of Derek Morgan, who at times seemed to dictate events almost at will. The outstanding player for Overseas was 'Taffy' Grey, forward, who scored two tries. It was a fitting finale to an enjoyable season.

Arrangements were made by Mr. R. Squires, with the kind permission of Surgeon Captain McLintock, R.N., Royal Naval Hospital, Bighi, for members of the touring teams in Malta over the Easter period to be treated as out-patients and hospitalized in the R.N. Hospital. This service was free under a 'Grace and Favour' scheme.

And so ended perhaps the most memorable playing season of all. Amongst the memories of the normal Saturday and Sunday games, one thinks of two very pretty girls who took the oranges on at half time, Judith Cross and Maureen Jones, they too were part of this season. Also Arthur Cross who took his duties as Trainer on the afternoon of a game very seriously. The Cup for the 'Most Improved Player' was not presented for the 1967/68 season.

Social Events 1967/68

The Social life of the Club during the 1967/68 season was very full and very successful. With three sides to entertain over the Easter period, a Sub-Committee was formed in January, 1968, to organize fund raising and entertainment. Initially, the Sub-Committee members were Messrs P. Parker (Chairman), B.S. Jackson; T.N. Ricketts; C. Rice and C. Brown. Unfortunately, Peter Parker was away from Malta for 7 weeks at the vital time and Colin Brown took over as Hon. Social Secretary and Chairman of the Sub-Committee.

Various functions were organized to raise cash. Members who made the trip to Messina were asked to pay £4. each into the Tour Fund. All members of the Club were asked to donate to the Tour Fund and their donations were added to a Subscription List. The General Committee agreed that the Overseas Club should pay up to £100 towards tour expenses, and that any excess in fund raising to be kept in reserve for future tours.

The hard work by Colin Brown and his Sub-Committee, Social and General Committees, and the response to all appeals and functions organized by the Committees was tremendous and helped to assure the success of the Tours to Malta. Quoting from the President, Mr. J.P. Ratcliffe, 'Of course, tours of this kind cannot exist without organization, and here I would like to put on record my very sincere thanks to those members of the Committee who gave up so much of their time to help. Our Secretary, Norman Morgan, who organized both our trips to Sicily and the return visits. The Special Social Committee of Colin Brown, Trevor Ricketts, Charlie Rice, and Bar Manager, Brian Jackson, did a remarkable job of fund raising between Christmas and Easter. Without all this work and goodwill, the tours would not just have been flops - they would have been impossible.
