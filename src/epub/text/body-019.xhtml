The game was being reffed by a Major who was the family doctor at Mtarfa Hospital. Doc, complete with his attache case, rushed onto the field and proceeded to stick a needle in. The Major was aghast, and apparently looked at Doc in horror asking him who he thought he was and what he thought he was doing. Doc reckons he saved my life, bless him. This story has been told to me by three ex members of the day. The other story is that on one occasion Doc decided he was ill, made a chit out for himself, and off he went to Bighi Hospital. Jimmy Gray and one or two other Overseas members visited him and they were told that no such person was in hospital. Eventually he was found in the seaman's ward and duly transferred to a private ward. Doc Doyle would have said he was happier in a seaman's ward.

During these early years there were many good players but I think the two most worthy of note are the English internationals, R. Stirling, and D.T. Wilkins. Wilky was a Fleet Air Arm pilot on board the aircraft carrier H.M.S. Ocean, and Bob Stirling was a Fl. Lt. in the R.A.F. both serving in Malta in approximately 1948. They both played for England, 1951-53, and Bob Stirling played a final year when he Captained England in 1953/54. Men off H.M.S. Ocean gave Overseas a great deal of support both on the field and socially.

Other members well remembered from this period, not necessarily in order of joining the Club, and not previously mentioned are as follows:-
T. Percy, Grimhoff, Anderton, L. Lees, D. Gillard, Riley, K. Nichols, Montgomery, Jock Muir, S. Bucktan, T. Parry, P. Perry, Stewart, L. Bellamy, T. Healey.
