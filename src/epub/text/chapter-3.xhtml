The Formation of Overseas R.F.C.

The idea of forming a civilian rugby club in 1946 was first mooted by sportsmen who were attached to, or associated with, what was then the naval 'Dockyard', and details of its beginning come mainly from two of its founder members, Mr. Trevor Lewis and Lt. Cdr. R. Meredith (Retd). Trevor claims to be the original member of Overseas. A civilian with Admiralty, he arrived in Malta in February, 1946, and immediately started looking for a game of rugby. Having no team of his own, he haunted the Marsa in hopes that teams would arrive short. Eventually, he spoke to the Commodore Superintendent of the Dockyard, Commodore D. Orr-Ewing, on the subject, and the Commodore suggested that Trevor form his own rugby team. This was the beginning. With the complete backing and authority of Commodore Orr-Ewing, signals were sent to Dockyard, Civilian and Service Units querying initial support for starting a rugby club. There was an immediate response from 20 to 30 interested sportsmen, including Mr. Fred Hitchcock, who, with Trevor, became one of the inaugurating Officials of the Club.

Ray Meredith arrived in Malta in March, 1946, and as an Instructor Lieutenant, R.N., he was appointed to re-open H.M. Dockyard Children's School in two semi-detached villas, 'Sunshine' and 'Sea Foam', at Ta' Xbiex. Ray tells of an informal meeting held towards the end of 1946 in the bar of Queens Hall, Old College Street, Sliema, when a group of cricket and tennis players discussed forming a Dockyard Rugby Club. I assume that this informal meeting was the response to Trevor's initial signals. It was then realised that there would not be sufficient playing membership in the Dockyard and it was decided to call a meeting of all interested in playing rugby, to include all U.K. and Commonwealth personnel who were working in Malta, and who were unattached to Service Units.

The formation meeting called was the first Annual General Meeting of the Club although at this stage it would be premature to call it the 'Overseas'. This first A.G.M. was held in November, 1946, in the A.S.V. (Admiralty Salvage Vessels) Club in Regent House, Tower Road, Sliema. Regent House stood at the junction of Tigne Street and Tower Road and was pulled down in 1953 to make way for Bisazza Street. The entrance to Regent House was approached up an open and fairly wide iron staircase. This meeting place was described in some detail by Trevor and also confirmed by Ray. I personally remember Regent House, although I did not know of its connection with Overseas at the time. In the local newspaper 'Times of Malta', 25th October, 1946, an advert was inserted saying 'Regent House Sliema, Grand re-opening, Thursday, 31st October (1946), Select Dances and Variety Turns, formal dress optional', although this does not necessarily mean that the ASV Club was not in residence before or during renovation of Regent House. Approximately 25 men attended the formation meeting - 23 or so being prepared to play. Mr. Fred Hitchcock was elected as Chairman, a position he held with distinction until he left Malta in June, 1952. Ray describes the type of person who attended this meeting as 'personnel from the Yard, R.N., who came under the Commodore Superintendent of the Yard like himself, Land's Department, Dingli W/T station, and the odd body seconded to Malta for Army duties etc'.

The formation meeting was opened by Fred Hitchcock and Trevor Lewis, and on addressing those attending Fred outlined the general concept to form a Rugby Club of U.K. personnel or personnel attached to small units, civilians, etc. In Trevor's own words: 'From there the Club was formed'.

During this first season, although deeply involved in the Club's formation, Trevor Lewis declined all Offices. This was due to the fact that he lived at Mdina. However, he did serve on Committees, and with move of residence to Sliema he took up Office of Hon. Secretary for the two seasons, 1947/49. He left Malta in June, 1949.

Name of the Club

From the response received at the formation meeting it was decided that there was sufficient interest and playing strength to form a club. The name of the Club was discussed, and in view of the diversity of employment of the interested parties, it was decided that an all embracing title should be assumed. Both Trevor and Ray say that it was decided to call the Club the 'Overseas Rugby Football Club' and from then one would assume that it was at this first A.G.M. that the name 'Overseas' was decided upon. However, in my initial searches for information and old members to write to, I contacted the person who had owned and managed the Queens Hall, Old College Street, Sliema, a Mr. Paul Magro. From its earliest days the Club had held dances at the Queens Hall. Mr. Magro informed me that Lt. R. Meredith (R.N.), who used to live in a flat belonging to him, had arranged Overseas dances in the Queens Hall in 1947. Fortunately, Paul Magro had kept his old registers and we found that these dances, on different dates, were listed as being held by both the Overseas R.F.C. and the Dockyard R.F.C.. Mr. Magro confirmed that all the dances were organised by the same people. So, nothing loth, I contacted Mr. C.O. Borg (Lino to his associates) in the Office of Inland Revenue, Succession and other Duties Branch, Monte di Pieta Buildings, 46, Merchant Street, Valletta, as I had been informed that the Club would have had to apply there for a permit to hold entertainment at the Queens Hall. Lino was exceptionally helpful and the resulting information is given below. It would suggest that initially there was some confusion over the name of the Club.

The dance held on the 17th March, 1947, was on St. Patrick's night and an article was inserted in the Sunday Times of Malta on the 23rd March, 1947. This did nothing to clear the matter as it said that the dance was organised by the 'Rugger Club' by Lt. Cdr. Meredith who also acted as M.C. On the other hand, the 2nd A.G.M. of the Club, which was held at the Queens Hall on the 7th October, 1947, was also registered with the Inland Revenue Office, presumably for a spirit licence. On this occasion the Club applying for the licence was given as the 'Overseas Rugby Club', and it would appear to confirm that, from this date at least, the Club was officially known by this name. This is partially borne out by details given to me by Mr. Lino Borg appertaining to dances. When I joined the Club in November, 1948, there was no suggestion that the Club had been known as anything but 'Overseas R.F.C.'.

The First President

Commodore D. Orr-Ewing, Overseas' first President, was deeply involved in setting the Club on its feet in this first season, making arrangements to provide pitch, posts, flags, strip, etc. He provided the 'physicals' whilst Trevor Lewis, initially on his own, then with Mr. Fred Hitchcock and the Club Committee, brought the team together. The Commodore left Malta on the 19th December, 1947, after serving there for nearly two years. Unfortunately, he died in 1964, but his son, Mr. C. Orr-Ewing, kindly supplied details of the Commodore's rugby playing days. It is very impressive. He was a Cambridge Blue in 1919, played for the Royal Navy a number of times, played for Hampshire and was a Scottish trialist. Also, when he was in South Africa, he played for a team called the Villagers.

Mr. A.R.F. Wright, the Rugby Football Union historian, was more specific about Commodore Orr-Ewing's naval rugby record. He told me that the Commodore represented the Royal Navy in Inter-Service matches from 1920 to 1926 inclusive, and again in 1930, also that he played for the United Services Portsmouth. Further research by Mr. Wright resulted in his finding a 1964 Harlequin F.C. member's list in which the Commodore was recorded as being a Life Member of the Club. Mr. Wright was also able to provide the following brief history of the 'Villager R.F.C.', the team for which the Commodore played in South Africa:

"The Villager R.F.C. is a Club founded in 1876 in Western Province, South Africa. Service men from the British Isles have played for this Club including W.G.E. Luddington (R.N. and England) and H.W. Stephenson (R.N. and Ireland)."

The First Chairman

Mr. F. Hitchcock, Chairman, was one of the mainstays of Overseas from its inception to the time he left Malta in June, 1952. In fact, it has been generally believed over the years that he was the Founder Member. Almost true, but not quite. When starting research into the Club's beginning, I myself believed Mr. Hitchcock to be the initiator of the Club and attempted to trace him. This was to lead to a dead end as far as the Club's history was concerned, but it resulted in gathering very interesting information as regards rugby in Malta pre-war. Fred Hitchcock had been a U.K. Based Industrial (Foreman Electrician) in H.M. Dockyard, Malta, and armed with initial information from Montgomery House, Floriana, Malta, I wrote to Defence Accounts Office, Worcester, England. The DAO informed me that Fred had died in August, 1958. However, from two other sources I was informed of his possible connection with the pre-war "Exiles" and "E.R.A." club(s), and the possible link between these club(s) and Overseas. When referred to appertaining to the pre-war period, the two clubs "Exiles" and "E.R.A.", always seemed tied together as one club. Further enquiries proved that no connection existed between Overseas and the Exiles/E.R.A.s, but I believe readers will be interested in the information gathered.

First, Commander E. Hamilton-Hill R.N. (Retd) informed me that the formation of Overseas Rugby Club stemmed from the run-down of the Exiles (Cable & Wireless) and the E.R.A. Club which used to run its own rugger side. At the same time the Commander stressed that his was a hazy recollection. Lt. Cmdr R. Meredith informed me that Mr. Hitchcock was the oldest member at the meeting (November, 1946) and was a former playing member of the pre-war Exiles and E.R.A. Rugby Clubs. In further correspondence Lt. Cmdr Meredith shed more light on who the Exiles and E.R.A.s were by saying, "Mr. Hitchcock played for the 'Exiles' E.R.A.'s in Dockyard Appointments and that Cable & Wireless were the mainstay of the Club, together with Naval Officers on posts like the R.N. School and the Dockyard Technical College". I do know that the E.R.A. Rugby Club existed in Malta, as a separate Club, at least from after the war up to the 1954/55 season. Overseas played the E.R.A. club twice during the 1954/55 season, after which there is no further record of the E.R.A.s as rugby opponents.

In following up the "Exiles" clue, I first contacted Officials at what was formerly Cable & Wireless, Mercury House, St. George's Road, St. Julians, Malta. Cable & Wireless in Malta became Telemalta Corporation early in 1975. Here I was given the greatest help by Messrs. G.A. Cassar Trigona, M.Bonnici, A. Tortell and a former member of Cable & Wireless Staff, Mr. R. Scerri. Between them they gave me the backgrounds to the Exiles Club, Malta. Mr. Scerri, an all round sportsman as a younger man and Malta tennis champion for many years, remembered Cable & Wireless "Exiles" rugby team. He advised me to write to the Exiles Club, London, and to a Mr. A. J. Hatfield who had been a playing member of the Exiles rugby team and prominent in the organisation of the Exiles Club, Malta, in the 1920's. The Exiles Club, London, had no records or photos which would help, and none of the Club members who had been in Malta in the 20's could help answer my queries. However, corresponding with Mr. Hatfield gave me the following interesting information, and I copy his own words:

"The Exiles Club (London) was formed by the Eastern Telegraph Company in 1921 and from that date E.T.C. sides played team games in all parts of the world. My own experience of rugger in Malta was from 1925 to 1927. We had a good side and could hold our own with such battleships as the 'Iron Duke', 'Valiant', 'Empress of India', 'Queen Elizabeth' and so on and in 1927 we won the Command Cup, defeating the R.A.F. Kalafrana, Border Regiment and, in the final, the Kings Own (Liverpool) Regiment. But from that time automation caught up with us and our cable staff dwindled sadly so that we were unable to raise any more teams. Personally, I moved on to Suez and Aden where we still maintained strong teams until 1929/30 when we amalgamated with the Marconi Company, Post Office and sundry other companies to form Cable & Wireless."

"When I returned to Malta in 1936 I found that the Exiles Club could produce no rugby team of its own and the Club could only make a showing at tennis - at which Mr. Robert Scerri excelled."

"So, I think I can say quite definitely that the Exiles Club had no hand in the formation of the Overseas Rugby Club. We never raised a rugby team in Malta after 1927. I am afraid I didn't know Mr. Hitchcock and I do not think he was connected with the Exiles Club, as membership was restricted to Cable Company's staff."

"We played at the Marsa Club of which we were all members. The reason for this was that the Eastern Telegraph Company owned all the land that is now the Polo Ground. This land was presented to the Marsa Club on the condition that all U.K. based members of the Company should be automatic members of the Marsa Club and Tigne Bathing Club. That must have been some time after 1868 when the first cable was laid to Malta. The name "Exiles" was not used until about 1921 when the Club was formed in London and branches abroad took the same name. I think 1921 to 1927 about covers our rugger activities in Malta although we ran soccer teams considerably earlier under the name E.T.C."

The above information from Mr. Hatfield would prove that any amalgamation of the Exiles Club and the E.R.A. Club, and therefore the period when Mr. Hitchcock was a playing member of same, could possibly only have been between 1927 and 1936, i.e., when Mr. Hatfield was away from Malta. Otherwise, surely Mr. Hatfield would have known of any such amalgamation. Mr. Hitchcock was 40 years of age in 1936. Unfortunately, I do not know when he first served in Malta.

Overseas Colours

The first jerseys used by Overseas in 1946/47 were naval ratings summer shirts, tough linen, and provided by Commodore Orr-Ewing. The same season a set of jerseys, navy blue and white hoops, together with stockings and balls, were purchased through NAAFI. Navy blue and white have continued through the years to be the traditional colours of Overseas XVs. Ray Meredith informs me that the money, raised by holding dances, paid for this equipment, and at the same time helped the Club to become solvent.

First Season

Training sessions for at least the first season were held twice a week, Tuesday and Thursday evenings, under lights on the tennis courts behind the Queens Hall, Old College Street. There was no coach and there was no organised training, it was a matter of fitness and occasional ball handling. Some dozen matches were played, January to March, 1947, with only one lost to the Air Arm Kalafrana. Ray Meredith has provided a team photo, taken behind the Queens Hall, for 1946/47.

Written Record of Overseas

Notices of the early life of the Club including its formation, social activities, fixtures and results, were published in Dockyard Temporary Memoranda (Malta). Unfortunately, all D.T.M.s were destroyed, possibly before, but definitely at the time of the British Forces withdrawal in 1972, and no record remains from this source. Likewise, all written records of 1946/47 kept by Trevor Lewis and Ray Meredith were disposed of before I wrote to them asking for details of the Club’s formation. Full searches were made through various records for any information about Overseas R.F.C., also for any connection between Overseas and clubs already mentioned, i.e. Dockyard, Exiles and ERA. All searches proved negative. A full list of departments and secretaries who carried out these searches on behalf of the Club have been given under ‘Acknowledgements to Non-Members’ at the front of this history.

Overseas XVs Home Ground

Initially, in 1946/47 Overseas played on a makeshift pitch at Ta’ Qali airfield which was soon abandoned in favour of a pitch at Luqa airfield. The Commodore and Trevor Lewis, and, I assume, with other club Officials, selected a piece of ground at Luqa, and the Commodore negotiated with R.A.F. Officials that this would become Overseas’ home pitch. This pitch was situated to the Luqa side of the runway, approximately in front of what is now the civilian terminal (1977). The pitch had a light covering of grass but it was hard, almost gravel, and aircraft often stopped play. The players changed in one of the nissen huts in R.A.F. Site one, Luqa, by courtesy of the R.A.F., although during 1949/50 season we were able to make use of the newly built and improved changing facilities at Luqa Site two. In those days very few of the players owned cars and a bus was hired from Captains Garage, Tigne Street, Sliema, to take them to the Site and then to the pitch. The team photo of 1948/49 shows the bus and Luqa Site number two airman’s quarters in the background.

Trevor Lewis informed me that during the first season the Commodore decided we must have our own pitch, and in conjunction with Officials from the Civil Engineering Department from the Dockyard, Overseas selected a piece of ground at Ta’ Qali and preparations were made. Ta’ Qali was a Naval Air Station at the time. It was agreed that the only type of grass was a special kind from Texas and this was delivered by courtesy of the U.S. Air Force en route to one of their bases. This pitch had to lay unused for 2 to 3 years in order to bind the grass. Trevor, who left Malta in June, 1949, does not know if we ever played on it. No record remains of this particular pitch, and no other member I have contacted, and who followed on after the first season, can remember hearing about it.

Overseas continued playing at Luqa during 1948/49 and for the first half of 1949/50 season, after which we moved to Ta’ Qali. It is difficult to be precise as to when we moved to Ta' Qali. I can confirm that we played at Luqa on the 27th November, 1949 against H.M.S. Falcon. On the 10th December, 1949, we played at Ta' Qali against the Med. Fleet. Also during the latter half of the 1948/49 season and the first half of the 1949/50 season we played once or twice at the Marsa, whether this was our home pitch, or the only pitch available at the time, I cannot be certain. During this period our away games against H.M.S. Falcon, the Fleet Air Arm Team, were played at Hal Far. At this time the runway was still in use and we occasionally had to take down the goal posts during a game when aircraft landed.

The use of the pitch at Ta' Qali started after Commander R.V. Brockman (now Vice-Admiral (Retd)) had scoured the island for a suitable piece of grass on which to play rugger. The area at Ta' Qali was decided on although it was hardly suitable as there were thousands of tiny shells. These were gradually cleared by the efforts of working parties, organised by Commander Martin Price R.N., the Commanding Officer at Ta' Qali, and the players.

New pitches were laid at Ta' Qali in 1952. It is recorded in the Times of Malta, 22nd November, 1952, that four full sized rugby football grounds had been constructed at R.N. Air Station (H.M.S. Goldfinch), Ta' Qali, to be shared with the R.A.F. and Army. By 1958/59 there were 6 full sized grounds at Ta' Qali allocated to different services. Two of these grounds were well laid near the perimeter road and were kept for Inter Service games and other special matches. The other four pitches were rougher, especially through continued use, but certainly an improvement on Luqa. The Australian Air Force was stationed at Ta' Qali from June, 1953 to approximately 1955, after which it was handed over to the R.A.F.

Overseas used Ta' Qali as their home ground from 1950 to 1960. From 1960 to the present date, our home ground has been at the Marsa. This final move was made when Marsa Lodge became our Club House. The rugby playing area at the Marsa was renovated in 1960 and the four pitches were known as Army 1 and 2 and Navy 1 and 2. Previously, the whole of the Marsa rugby playing area had been completely re-laid in late 1956.

Before the start of each rugby season Overseas received permission to use the pitches from the proper authority. This had been given by the Commanding Officer of the R.A.F. Station, Ta' Qali, and with the move to the Marsa, up to 1971/72, from the General Officer Commanding via the Secretary Army Rugby Football (Malta). In 1972 the whole of the Marsa playing area was taken over by the Maltese Government, and from this point we have played rugby at the Marsa by kind permission of the President of the Malta Sports Board.

During the early years, at least up to 1950, when the grounds were too hard to play rugby, the Overseas rugby team on the odd occasion played hockey against the 'rugby' opponent of the day. On the odd occasion we played against civilian clubs. Imagine 11 rugby players, only one or two with knowledge of hockey, wielding sticks. We never won but we scared the living daylights out of the opposition!!
