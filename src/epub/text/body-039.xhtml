After a difference of opinion between the Overseas Club and the Secretary of the J.S.S.B. in May, 1963, this method of payment of the Services' share of the profits was changed. The difference of opinion was due to the fact that the Secretary Army R.U. (Malta) had approached the Overseas Hon. Secretary before the end of the 1962/63 playing season and asked for an advance of approximately £40 to assist with the expenses incurred by the army rugby team which visited Tripoli early 1963. The Secretary J.S.S.B. objected on the grounds that Inter Service Sports Board had not been approached for, nor approved, this expenditure. All ended well, but from the 31st March, 1964 each Service representative was presented with an individual cheque for a third of the J.S.S.B.'s share of the profits.

The drop in profit for 1964 was due to a number of reasons including an increase in Bar Stewards wages, small profit margin and less consumption (impossible). The last two mentioned reasons were remedied.

During the 1961/62 season we obtained the services of Mr. Victor Dimech as a paid Bar Steward. Victor served the Club for 4 years before he left Malta on the 4th October, 1965 to live in U.K. He was succeeded as Bar Steward by Mr Angelo Galea who served the Club until it finally closed. In 1964 it is recorded that the Steward's wage was 3/- an hour and overtime as 4/- an hour. It is also recorded that the hours of opening were as follows:- Thursday, 7.30p.m. (Steward 7pm) – 12 midnight. If a Committee Member was present, and the Members present wished, The Club House could remain open until 1.30a.m. After midnight the Members present had to pay a levy of not less than a total of 6/- to pay the Steward. Wednesday. When requested by a Service team, 3p.m. to 7p.m. Saturday, 2.30p.m.(Steward 1.30p.m.) to 7p.m. Sunday, 4.30p.m. to 6.30p.m. Need I say that the Club rarely closed before 1.30a.m. on a Friday morning. One of the most hilarious games played on a Thursday night was liar dice. How the intellectuals at this game managed to plan and catch an unwary member two and a half times round the table I do not know. I also do not know how some of the schoolmasters taught the next morning - I believe they had some understanding pupils.

In 1964 it was estimated that something in the order of twenty fifteen made use of the Club House each week during the playing season. By this time the original furniture was worn out and sadly in need of replacement. In December, 1964, we purchased surplus furniture from the S.N.S.O., Naval Stores, Malta.

With the Club House and Bar being open three or four times a week during the playing season and with all labour being voluntary, except for the Bar Steward, it is obvious that there were problems in safeguarding the bar takings, especially during the busy periods on Wednesday and Saturday afternoons.
